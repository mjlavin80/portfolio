Data set manipulated using Python

Texas Department of Criminal Justice. "Executed Offenders." Death Row Information. N.p., n.d. Web. 21 Feb. 2017.

By manipulating this data, I made it easier for an audience to interpret it. Online, there are 10 total columns in the data set. That's a lot of information to interpret in one sitting. By narrowing it to one column -- just race -- I synthesized the information into something that was easier to comprehend and understand.

Example of how you made a choice related to visualization type


I would change this visualization to a pie chart so the number of each race on death row is more visually defined. As a bar graph, it looks like each race is equally represented out of the total number of offenders executed. As a pie chart, I think the numeric differences would be better defined since it would be represented as a percentage, not a total.

Example of how you made a choice related to color, etc.



Usually in heat maps, the darker the color, the more dense a population is on the map. The colors here are kind of random -- there's lots of green, purple and yellow -- so an audience might not be able to see the density of executed offenders in Texas per county. I would revise it by making it different opacities of the same color for experts, and I would add a legend with what each opacity means for those who aren't experts.

Comparing one thing to another


I chose this particular comparison of offenders executed per year and news events about Texas executions to see if there was a connection between what was happening outside of Texas death row and the number of people executed in the state. I think this comparison was effective because it showed the connection between the law and the number of people executed, but I wish I had more historical information from 1996 to 2005, because that part of the timeline has a large gap in it.
